  1. GENERAL


    1. INTRODUCTION


      1. The framework for performance-based building code is set out in the Building Control Regulations 2003 (referred to in this Document as the Regulations). The Fifth Schedule of the Regulations sets out the objectives and performance requirements that must be complied with in the design and construction of building works (referred to in this Document as “prescribed objectives and performance requirements”). The objectives set out community expectations of a safe, accessible and energy efficient building. The performance requirements outline the level of performance, which must be met in order for a building to meet the objectives.


      2. This Approved Document provides a set of ‘acceptable solutions’ that meet the prescribed objectives and performance requirements. The prescribed objectives and performance requirements are deemed to be satisfied if the design and construction of a building comply with the acceptable solutions.


      3. Alternatively, a person may utilise alternative solutions in respect of the design and construction of any building if these solutions satisfy the prescribed objectives and performance requirements. Alternative solutions are solutions that entail the use of any design, material or construction method that differs completely or partially from those in the acceptable solutions.

      1. A building, including its foundation, shall be designed and constructed so that the combined dead, imposed, wind and other intended loads can be sustained and transmitted by it to the ground –


        1. safely; and


        2. without causing such deflection or deformation of any part of that building, or such movement of the ground, as will impair the stability of any part of another building or property.

                2. without causing such deflection or deformation of any part of that structure, or such movement of the structure’s foundation, as will impair the stability of any part of another building or property.

              3. Tests that are appropriate to the materials and building works concerned shall be performed on those materials and building works.


              4. If a building or structure or any part thereof is to be demolished, the demolition works shall be carried out safely and without impairing the stability of any other part of that building or structure or another property.

    1. OBJECTIVE


      1. The objectives of paragraph C.2.1 are –

        1. to protect people from injury caused by inadequate headroom; and

        2. to prevent loss of amenity caused by inadequate height of room or space.


    2. PERFORMANCE REQUIREMENT


      1. Any room or space in a building must be provided with –

        1. adequate headroom; and

        2. adequate height,

          for the intended uses of the room or space.

        1. any attic that –

          1. does not exceed an area of 10 square metres; and

          2. is in a house that is built for the owner’s own use;


        2. any equipment or plant room;


        3. the underside of any staircase or escalator if the staircase or escalator is not located along an access route or circulation space;


        4. any toilet, bathroom or lavatory in any house built for the owner’s own use;


        5. any store room not exceeding an area of 6 square metres.


      3. The requirement in paragraph C.2.1(b) does not apply to any of the following rooms or spaces:


        1. any corridor or lobby;

        2. any toilet, bathroom, lavatory or powder room;

        3. any localised area within a room or space where there is a drop in ceiling height due to physical constraints such as structural beams or building services.

    1. ACCEPTABLE SOLUTION


      1. The requirement in paragraph C.2.1 is deemed to be satisfied if the specifications set out in paragraphs C.3.2 and C.3.3 are complied with.


      2. Headroom

            1. in the case of a doorway, the underside of the transom;

            2. in the case where a window opens into an access route or circulation space, the underside of the opened window, or

            3. in all other cases, the underside of any beam, duct, service pipe, fixture, fitting or other obstruction or projection.


          2. The headroom along a flight of staircase is measured vertically between the pitch line and any point directly above that limits the headroom. See Figure C.3.2.1(a) for illustration of headroom measurement at staircases.


          3. The pitch line is the notional line joining the leading edge or nosings (if any) of successive stair treads within a flight of a stairway.

      3. Ceiling height


        1. The ceiling height of rooms and spaces shall not be less than 2.4 metres.


          Note: The ceiling height is measured from the finished floor level to the underside of any slab, false ceiling or suspended ceiling, whichever is lower.
